This is a classic example of a 19th-century German church register (Kirchenbuch), specifically a marriage record from 1821. The headers are written in a mix of Fraktur (the printed typeface) and Kurrent (the cursive handwriting for the month).

**Divorce**: This explains the 12-year delay! Hardy was already a divorced man by 1810. Under the strict laws of the time, getting the official "permission to remarry" (zur anderweitigen Verehelichung zugelassen) after a divorce could be a bureaucratic nightmare, which likely explains why the couple lived together since 1809/1810 without a formal ceremony until 1821.

Occupation of Hardy's father: Hofschirrmeister. This is the exact same specialized title held by the bride's father (Eustachius Beck). It is very likely the two families knew each other through their professional circles in the royal or courtly service.

Location: He was a master blacksmith living near the Hallesches Tor (Halle Gate) in Berlin.

**Eustachius Beck**:  is a relatively rare Latinized German name. His title, Hofschirrmeister, suggests he was a high-ranking official in charge of the tack, wagons, or equipment for a noble court in Hattingen.


The record mentions they had already lived together for 12 years and had 3 children. In many Prussian provinces during this time, "Consens" (permission) to marry was often withheld by local authorities if the couple couldn't prove sufficient financial means.
Couples would often live together in a "provisional" state for years until they finally saved enough money or the laws relaxed, allowing them to officially "copulate" (marry) in the eyes of the church, thereby legitimizing their children.
